Why Use a Text Analysis Tool?

Every piece of writing has a hidden structure that affects how readers engage with it. Professional writers, editors, and marketers use text metrics to:

  • Hit word count targets for articles, essays, and social posts
  • Improve readability — simpler text reaches wider audiences
  • Check keyword balance — avoid over-optimization or under-use
  • Estimate engagement time — match reading time to audience attention span
  • Track vocabulary richness — avoid repetitive language

Key Metrics Explained

1. Word Count & Character Count

The foundation of any text analysis. Different platforms have different limits — Twitter (280 characters), Google Ads headlines (30 characters), meta descriptions (~160 characters), blog posts (1,500–2,500 words). Our tool gives you all counts at once.

2. Readability (Flesch-Kincaid Score)

The Flesch-Kincaid readability test scores text from 0–100. Higher scores mean easier reading:

ScoreLevelExample Publication
90–100Very EasyChildren's books
80–89EasyConsumer magazines
70–79Fairly EasyBusiness writing
60–69StandardThe New York Times
50–59Fairly DifficultThe Wall Street Journal
30–49DifficultAcademic journals
0–29Very DifficultLegal documents

Tip: For web content, aim for a score of 60–70 (Standard). This reaches the broadest audience while still sounding professional.

3. Keyword Density

Keyword density shows how often words appear relative to total word count. For SEO, the ideal keyword density is 1–3%. Too high and you risk "keyword stuffing" penalties. Too low and search engines may not understand your topic.

4. Reading Time & Speaking Time

Based on average reading speed (200 words/minute) and speaking speed (150 words/minute). These estimates help you plan content length for different formats:

  • Blog posts: 5–7 minute read (1,000–1,400 words)
  • Email newsletters: 3–5 minute read (600–1,000 words)
  • Product pages: Under 2 minutes (under 400 words)
